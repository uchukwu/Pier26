  1. Introduction‌

    The intricate interplay between geometric and topological concepts in quantum mechanics has unveiled profound connections between seemingly disparate phenomena. Among these, holonomy, Berry’s phase, and topological insulators stand as interconnected pillars of modern physics (Vanderbilt 2018).

    Additionally, the Ambrose-Singer theorem, a cornerstone of differential geometry, establishes a profound link between curvature and holonomy, paving the way for a deeper understanding of the geometric structure of space (Ambrose and Singer 1953). In turn, holonomy emerges as a central feature of Berry’s phase, a geometric phase acquired by a quantum system undergoing adiabatic evolution (Simon 1983).

    Berry’s phase, initially discovered in the context of the adiabatic theorem (Berry 1984), has transcended its roots to permeate various areas of physics, including topological insulators (C. Kane 2013). These exotic materials, characterized by a bandgap in their bulk but conducting states at their edges, harbor a wealth of topological properties, including a quantized Berry’s phase.

    The intricate interplay between these concepts has led to remarkable insights into the behavior of quantum systems, revealing the profound connections between geometry, topology, and quantum mechanics. Understanding these relationships is crucial for unraveling the mysteries of topological insulators and paving the way for novel technological applications.

    In section 2, we’ll describe holonomy, a geometric perspective on parallel transports. In section 3, we’ll delve into the interplay of the adiabatic theorem and Berry phase, and the connection of Berry phase to holonomy. In section 4, we’ll demonstrate application of the above tools for examining exotic electronic properties of topological insulators, through examining geometry of reciprocal lattice Brillouin zones via the associated Berry connection and curvature for determining topological invariants.


  2. Holonomy: A Geometric Perspective on Parallel Transport‌

    In the realm of differential geometry, holonomy emerges as a fundamental concept that captures the essence of parallel transport, a process of moving vectors along a path while preserving their geometric properties (Nakahara 2003). This intricate interplay between geometry and topology has profound implications in various branches of physics, including general relativity, quantum mechanics, and condensed matter physics.

    2. Mathematical Formalization‌

      To delve deeper into the mathematical underpinnings of holonomy, let’s introduce some essential concepts:


      1. Principal Bundles: A principal bundle is a fiber bundle equipped with a structure group that acts on the fibers in a particular way.

      2. Connections: A connection on a principal bundle provides a way to parallel transport geometric objects, such as vectors and tensors, along paths in the base manifold.

      3. Parallel Transport: Parallel transport is a process of moving geometric objects along paths while preserving their geometric properties, such as their magnitude and direction.

    3. Ambrose-Singer Theorem: A Cornerstone in Holonomy‌

      The Ambrose-Singer theorem, a pivotal result in differential geometry, establishes a profound link between the curvature of a principal bundle and the holonomy of a connection on that bundle. The theorem states that the holonomy group of a connection is determined by the curvature of the bundle.


      Proving the Ambrose-Singer Theorem


      The proof of the Ambrose-Singer theorem involves constructing a homomorphism between the Lie algebra of the holonomy group and the curvature tensor of the bundle. See Ambrose and Singer 1953 for proof. This homomorphism demonstrates the intrinsic relationship between these two geometric quantities.


      Applications of Holonomy in Physics



      Holonomy has permeated various areas of physics, offering valuable insights into the behavior of physical systems:

      1. General Relativity: In general relativity, holonomy plays a crucial role in understanding the geometry of spacetime (Nakahara 2003). The holonomy of the spin connection on the tangent bundle of a spacetime is intimately connected to the curvature of the spacetime.

      2. Quantum Mechanics: In quantum mechanics, holonomy emerges as a key ingredient in Berry’s phase, a geometric phase acquired by a quantum system undergoing adiabatic evolution (Simon 1983). This phase, influenced by the holonomy of the Berry connection, has profound implications in diverse quantum phenomena.

      3. Condensed Matter Physics: In condensed matter physics, holonomy holds the key to unlocking the mysteries of topological insulators (Vanderbilt 2018). These materials, characterized by a bandgap in their bulk but conducting states at their edges, harbor intriguing topological properties, including a quantized Berry’s phase, intimately linked to the holonomy of the Berry connection.

        Holonomy, a cornerstone of differential geometry, has transcended its mathematical roots to become an indispensable tool in various branches of physics. Understanding the intricate interplay between holonomy, geometry, and topology is crucial for paving the way for novel technological advancements and unraveling the mysteries of the universe, such as the geometric tapestry of the adiabatic theorem.


  3. Adiabatic Theorem and Berry Phase‌

    The adiabatic theorem is a fundamental concept in quantum mechanics that describes the behavior of a quantum system when its Hamiltonian changes slowly over time (Dam, Mosca, and Vazirani 2001). The theorem states that if a quantum system is initially in an eigenstate of its Hamiltonian, and the Hamiltonian is changed slowly enough, then the system will remain in an eigenstate of the Hamiltonian at all times.

    Related to that, the Berry phase is a geometric phase that arises in the adiabatic evolution of a quantum system (Urbantke 1991). It is a phase factor that is acquired by the wavefunction of the system as it is transported around a closed loop in parameter space (Figure 3). The Berry phase is related to the curvature of the parameter space, and it can be expressed in terms of the Berry connection.

    2. Berry Phase‌

      The geometric phase factor acquired by a quantum state during cyclic evolution in parameter space is the Berry phase (Figure 4). Consider a parameter space with coordinates {θ1, θ2, ..., θn}, and let |ψn(θ)⟩ be the eigenstate of the Hamiltonian H(θ) with eigenvalue En(θ). If we adiabatically transport the system along a closed loop in parameter space, the state |ψn(θ)⟩ may acquire a phase known as the Berry phase, given by:

      γn = −i I ⟨ψn(θ)| ∇θ |ψn(θ)⟩ · dθ, (2)

      where ∇θ is the gradient operator with respect to the parameters θ, and the integral is taken over a closed loop in parameter space. In fact, the integrand,

      −i ⟨ψn(θ)| ∇θ |ψn(θ)⟩, is the Berry connection, and is denoted as An(θ).



      Figure 4: Schematic representation of Berry phase on the complex projective space CPN−1 (the Riemannian sphere in this instance), denoted as CPN−1 = H0/U (1), where H0 is the Hilbert space and U (1) represents the one-dimensional unitary group acting on H0. The space R0 is the quotient ray space H0/U (1), composed of equivalence classes of Hilbert space vectors related by a nonzero complex scalar factor. A curve C, described by the physical state of the system on R0, can have multiple liftings into the space of unitary vectors, and the liftings illustrate any geometric phase acquired by the quantum system as it completes a closed path. For a given curve, the difference in the initial and final points (e.g. - curve C1) of the lifted curve signifies the nontrivial Berry phase accumulated during the cyclic evolution.


    3. Curvature and Connection‌

      As shown, the Berry phase γn acquired by a quantum state |ψn(θ)⟩ undergoing cyclic evolution in parameter space is given by the line integral of the Berry connection An(θ) along the closed loop in parameter space. Further, the Berry connection is intimately related to the curvature of a quantum system’s parameter space, referred to as the Berry curvature, and it plays a crucial role in the geometric phase acquired by a quantum state undergoing cyclic evolution (Simon 1983).

      Mathematically, the Berry curvature Fn associated with the Berry connection An(θ) is given by the curl (or exterior derivative) of the Berry connection:


      Fn = ∇θ × An(θ). (3)

      where ∇θ × represents the curl with respect to parameter space θ.



      The Berry curvature is a measure of how much the Berry connection changes as one moves in parameter space. It encodes geometric information about the quantum states and their evolution.

      One of the key results in this context is the Stokes theorem, which relates the Berry phase γn acquired by a quantum system undergoing cyclic evolution to the integral of the Berry curvature over the enclosed surface in parameter space (Nakahara 2003):

      γn = I An(θ) · dθ = ∫ ∫ Fnd2θ (4)

      This relation emphasizes that the Berry phase is determined by the integral of the Berry curvature over the enclosed surface in parameter space. The curvature captures the nontrivial geometric features of the parameter space, and it is a crucial aspect of the geometric phase in quantum mechanics.

      In condensed matter physics, the Berry curvature is related to topological invariants and plays a significant role in understanding topological phases of matter, such as the quantum Hall effect and topological insulators (Hasan and C. L. Kane 2010).


  4. Topological Insulators: A Realm of Exotic Electronic Properties‌

    In the realm of condensed matter physics, topological insulators stand as a class of materials that exhibit remarkable electronic properties, characterized by an insulating bulk and conducting states along their edges, defying conventional notions of conductivity (Hasan and C. L. Kane 2010). These intriguing materials have garnered immense attention due to their potential applications in quantum computing, spintronics, and other advanced technologies.


    1. Reciprocal Lattice and Brillouin Zone in Topological Insulators‌

      At the heart of topological insulators’ unique properties lies the interplay between topology, geometry, and quantum mechanics. In the context of topological insulators, the parameter space corresponds to the Brillouin zone, the region in reciprocal space that encompasses the periodic structure of the material (Vanderbilt 2018). The Brillouin zone (BZ) is a fundamental concept intimately connected to the electronic band structure and topological invariants (Figure 8). The Brillouin zone and the associated Berry connection play a crucial role in determining the topological properties of electronic states in topological insulators.

      Consider a topological insulator described by a periodic crystal lattice with a reciprocal lattice described by vectors {Gn}. The Brillouin zone is defined as the Wigner-Seitz cell of this reciprocal lattice, and it encapsulates the entire reciprocal lattice (Radaelli 2011).

    2. Berry Connection and Topological Invariants‌

      The Berry connection (A(k)) and Berry curvature (F (k)) are key geometric quantities associated with electronic states in the Brillouin zone (Vanderbilt 2018).


      1. Berry connection A(k): The Berry connection is a non-abelian gauage potential associated with the eigenstates |ψn(k)⟩ of the Hamiltonian at a specific point k in the Brillouin zone:


        A(k) = −i ⟨ψn(k)| ∇k |ψn(k)⟩ (7) It guides the motion of the wavefunction along paths in parameter space.

      2. Berry curvature F(k): The Berry curvature is the curl of the Berry

        connection:

        F (k) = ∇k × A(k) (8)

        It quantifies how the Berry connection changes as k varies in the Brillouin zone. It describes the curvature of the parameter space.

    4. Concluding Insights: The Unraveling of the Geometric Tapestry of Topological Insulators‌

      In conclusion, the study of topological insulators has unveiled a rich interplay between geometric concepts and electronic band structures, manifesting in the form of topological invariants. These invariants, such as the Z2 invariant, Chern number, TKNN invariant, and Fu-Kane-Mele invariant, serve as powerful descriptors of the nontrivial topological phases exhibited by these materials. Crucially, these invariants find their roots in geometric quantities like Berry connection, Berry curvature, and Berry phase, which reflect the evolution of electronic states in momentum space. The first Brillouin zone, acting as the geometric scaffold, encapsulates the entire reciprocal lattice, and the associated geometric phases become fundamental in characterizing the robust, topologically protected electronic states observed in topological insulators. This intricate interconnection between topology and geometry provides a profound understanding of the unique electronic properties of topological insulating materials, offering a promising avenue for exploring novel quantum phenomena with potential applications in future electronic devices.

    1. Principal Bundles‌

      Definition 5.1. A principal bundle, P , is a topological and geometric structure composed of a fiber bundle F over a base space X, with a structure group G. P consists of the following properties:

      1. A projection π from P onto X, shown as π : P → X in Figure 6.

      2. A continuous right action P × G → P ; example shown in Figure 7b as

        pg ∈ (G → P ), where p ∈ Px and g ∈ G.

        The action of G preserves the fibers, and acts freely and transitively on F in

        P . Hence, there exists an homeomorphism between G and each fiber of the principal bundle.

        One example of a principal bundle is in the category of smooth manifolds, where π is a smooth map between smooth manifolds. The structure group G for this principal bundle is a Lie group and the continuous right action on P is required to be smooth.
