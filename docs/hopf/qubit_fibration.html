This paper is a review of quantum bits (qubits), a two-level quantum system,
using tools of Hopf ﬁbration. Hopf ﬁbration is a geometric and topological con-
struction that provides a way to represent a three-dimensional sphere (3-sphere)
using complex geometry and ﬁber bundles (Hopf 1931). In standard norm, an
n-sphere is described by a set of points in an (n+1)-dimensional space at a ﬁxed
distance from a central point. The distance of the points on the sphere from
the central point can be assumed to be of unit length, meaning for a point
designated as (x

Furthermore, the total space of a qubit is a two-dimensional complex space,
known as the Hilbert space. Through mapping the Hilbert space of a qubit to a
ﬁber bundle (Urbanke 1991), which is a mathematical framework for describing
how spaces are locally modeled by a common structure, a geometrical structure
is prescribed for associating one topological space (the base space) with another
space (the ﬁber space) in a consistent and continuous manner. The base space
(the qubit’s 2-dimensional Bloch sphere, which is a geometrical representation
1
of the state space of a two-level quantum mechanical system), denoted as B, is
the primary/background space, and the ﬁber space (the qubit’s global phase),
denoted as F , is a space of ﬁbers associated with each point on the base space
(see Figure 2 for visualization of ﬁbration).
Thus, the total space, denoted as E, is a copy of the global phase ﬁber
space at each point of the Bloch sphere base space, combined in a smooth and
consistent manner. Furthermore, a projection map, denoted as π, is provided
as a continuous map from the total space to the Bloch sphere base space. It
associates each point in the total space with the corresponding point in the
Bloch sphere. Such projection map is required to satisfy the property that for
each point in the Bloch sphere, the inverse image under the projection map is
homeomorphic to the ﬁber space. In simple terms, the ﬁber over each point in
the Bloch sphere base space looks like the ﬁber space itself.
In this paper, we ﬁrst provide a description, mathematically and conceptu-
ally, of Hopf ﬁbration. Next, we provide a description of the projection map to
the Bloch sphere, the state space representation of a single qubit.
